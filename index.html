About Dublin History Quizzes

"Our architectural heritage is a unique resource, an irreplaceable expression of the richness and diversity of our past. Structures and places can, over time, acquire character and special interest through their intrinsic quality, continued existence and familiarity. The built heritage consists not only of great artistic achievements, but also of the everyday works of craftsmen. In a changing world, these structures have a cultural significance which we may recognise for the first time only when individual structures are lost or threatened. As we enjoy this inheritance, we should ensure it is conserved in order to pass it on to our successors."

- Architectural Heritage Protection Guidelines (DEHLG)

Awareness of our shared heritage is crucial to ensuring its continued survival, but it is frequently the case that people do not know about the history of their local areas, often passing by historical sites on their own doorsteps.

Even for those who want to learn, it can be difficult to know where to start or to access information without becoming overwhelmed. A number of organisations run out-reach and heritage education programmes with schools, but resources can be limited.

This website is intended to illustrate a template for engaging second-level students with the history of their local areas. While the quizzes on this website relate to Dublin City, the model for the website can be adapted to any area, regardless of scale. The quizzes are intended to get students thinking about the development of their local areas as a first step towards deeper engagement with heritage.

Resources

Would you like to learn more about local history? Here is a list of some helpful resources for people of all ages:

  • For more information on heritage in your local area, Local Authorities often provide inform and hold events on local heritage. For example, Dun Laoghaire-Rathdown County Council provide a list of links to resources about Dun Laoghaire's heritage, including virtual tours. To find your Local Authority, visit the Local Government Ireland website.
  • Most heritage organisations, like ICOMOS Ireland, the Irish Georgian Society, An Taisce, the Industrial Heritage Association, the Historic Houses of Ireland, the Dublin Civic Trust, etc. hold events on heritage and tours of historic properties and areas.
  • The Discovery Programme Centre for Archaeology and Innovation Ireland provides a wide range of resources for all age groups, such as Discovering the Bronze Age Resource Pack for Teachers
  • The Heritage Council's Heritage In Schools programme website provides a wide range of resources for teaching children about their local natural, cultural, archaeological and architectural heritage.
  • UNESCO provides a World Heritage in Young Hands Kit for teachers to help raise awareness and appreciation among children for local and world heritage.

John Speed's 'Map of Dubline'

John Speed's 'Map of Dubline' of 1610 is the oldest surviving map of Dublin City, this version of the map being a reprint of 1896. The map was included in John Speed's atlas 'The Theatre of the Empire of Great Britaine' published in 1611. Speed likely never visited Ireland so this map was probably put together from other sources, perhaps older maps, which have since been lost.

As well as Dublin Castle (construction began in 1204), St Patrick's Cathedral (then still St Patrick's Church, originally constructed between 1220 and 1259) and St Michan's Church (established in 1085 and rebuilt in 1685), there are many famous Dublin sites shown on the map. These include Trinity College (founded 1592), Christchurch (founded around 1030) and Dublin's City walls (parts of which still remain, such as the sections near St Audoen's Church). King's Inns, which was established 1541 and shown as 'the Innes', was located on the site of the existing Four Courts on Speed's Map (it later moved to its current location on Constitution Hill during the 1790s). Speed's map also shows that the layout of several streets has largely survived to this day, included Wood Quay, Fishamble Street, Winetavern Street, Merchant's Quay, Dame Street, South Great George's Street, Thomas Street and St James Gate.
